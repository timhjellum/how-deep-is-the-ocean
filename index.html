Zone 1 of 5

Sunlight Zone

0 – 200 M · PHOTOSYNTHESIS POSSIBLE

Every food web in the ocean starts here. Sunlight drives photosynthesis, plankton bloom, and nearly all marine life passes through this layer at some point in its life.

🐬

Common Dolphin

0–50 m

Surfaces to breathe every few minutes; dives briefly to hunt schooling fish near the surface.

🐠

Reef Fish

0–40 m

Reef-associated species cluster in the brightest, warmest water where coral can grow.

🪼

Moon Jellyfish

0–20 m

Drifts with the current, pulsing gently, translucent enough to let sunlight pass straight through.

🐢

Green Sea Turtle

0–100 m

Grazes on sunlit seagrass meadows and surfaces every few minutes to breathe air.

🦈

Whale Shark

0–200 m

The largest fish alive, filter-feeding on plankton concentrated near the sunlit surface.

🐦

Flying Fish

0–10 m

Builds speed underwater, then glides above the surface on wing-like fins to escape predators.

Zone 2 of 5

Twilight Zone

200 – 1,000 M · LIGHT FADES TO BLACK

Sunlight dims to a blue-grey haze and vanishes by 1,000 m. No plants grow here. Most residents make their own light — the first bioluminescence appears in this layer.

How far humans have gone, unassisted by a hull
🫁

Deepest Free Dive

214 m

Herbert Nitsch, No-Limits Apnea, Spetses, Greece, 2007. A single breath, a weighted sled down, and an inflatable lift bag back up.

🤿

Deepest Scuba Dive

332.35 m

Ahmed Gabr, Red Sea, Egypt, 2014. A 12-minute descent followed by roughly 13 hours of decompression stops on the way back up.

⚓

US Navy Submarine

~200 m

Publicly disclosed maximum operating depth for a Los Angeles-class attack submarine. Actual test and crush depths are classified.

🦑

Humboldt Squid

200–700 m

Hunts in coordinated packs, flashing red and white body patterns to communicate in the dark.

🐟

Lanternfish

300–800 m

The most abundant vertebrate on Earth by biomass; uses light-producing organs to blend into faint downwelling light.

🗡️

Hatchetfish

200–600 m

Its flattened, silvered body scatters light from above, camouflaging its silhouette from predators below.

🐡

Bristlemouth

400–1000 m

Migrates upward each night to feed near the surface, then retreats to the dark by day.

Zone 3 of 5

Midnight Zone

1,000 – 4,000 M · PERPETUAL DARKNESS

Total darkness, near-freezing water, and crushing pressure. Food is scarce, falling only as "marine snow" — the slow drift of organic debris from above. Life here is patient, slow-moving, and often self-luminous.

Resting in this zone
🚢

Wreck of the Titanic

~3,800 m

Discovered in 1985 on the floor of the North Atlantic, roughly 2.4 km deeper than any scuba or free-dive record — reachable only by submersible or ROV.

🎣

Anglerfish

1,000–2,000 m

Dangles a glowing lure grown from its own spine to draw prey directly to its jaws.

🐍

Gulper Eel

1,000–3,000 m

An enormous hinged jaw lets it swallow prey far larger than its own body in a food-scarce world.

🦑

Vampire Squid

600–1,200 m

Neither squid nor octopus; survives on falling detritus instead of hunting live prey.

🐙

Dumbo Octopus

1,000–4,000 m

Ear-like fins let it hover just above the seafloor, among the deepest-living octopuses known.

Zone 4 of 5

Abyssal Zone

4,000 – 6,000 M · THE ABYSSAL PLAINS

Flat, cold, and near-freezing at 2–3°C, the abyssal plains cover more of Earth's surface than any other single habitat. Pressure here exceeds 400 atmospheres. Life survives on scraps and, near vents, on chemical energy instead of sunlight.

🥒

Sea Cucumber

4,000–6,000 m

Grazes slowly across the seafloor sediment, digesting organic material settled from above.

🕷️

Sea Spider

4,000–5,000 m

Grows unusually large in the cold abyss, walking the seafloor on impossibly thin legs.

🐡

Tripod Fish

4,000–5,000 m

Props itself on rigid fin rays like a tripod, facing the current to ambush drifting food.

Zone 5 of 5 — Final Descent

Hadal Zone

6,000 – 10,935 M · OCEAN TRENCHES

Named for Hades. This zone exists only inside deep-sea trenches, where the seafloor itself plunges downward. Pressure exceeds 1,000 atmospheres — over 1,000 kg pressing on every square centimeter. Remarkably, life still holds on.

🐟

Hadal Snailfish

6,000–8,200 m

The deepest fish ever recorded, with a soft, gelatinous body built to withstand extreme pressure.

🦐

Giant Amphipod

6,000–10,900 m

A scavenging crustacean found at nearly every depth of the trench, including the very bottom.

🦠

Xenophyophore

8,000–10,600 m

A single giant cell, among the largest of its kind, found resting directly on the trench floor.

10,935 M

Challenger Deep, Mariana Trench — the deepest known point in any ocean on Earth. Sunlight last reached this water roughly a thousand meters above.
